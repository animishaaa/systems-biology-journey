# Why do I want to become a Systems Biologist?

My journey toward systems biology began during one of Professor Zelmina's lectures, when we discussed Yuri Lazebnik's famous article, *"Can a Biologist Fix a Radio?"*.

The article compares the way engineers diagnose a broken radio with the way biologists often study living systems. Although written with humor and satire, it raises an important scientific question: *Can we truly understand a complex biological system by studying only its individual components?*

This paper changed the way I think about biology.

In engineering, diagnosing a faulty radio involves comparing it with many working and damaged radios, understanding how each component interacts with the others, and identifying which failure causes the observed behavior. Biology is even more complex. Cells, tissues, organs, genes, proteins, metabolites, and signaling pathways constantly interact, forming networks rather than isolated parts.

That realization sparked my curiosity. I became fascinated by the idea that understanding biology requires studying interactions between systems instead of focusing on a single molecule or pathway.

That was the moment I decided that I wanted to become a systems biologist.

This environment will continuously push me to learn new technologies, computational methods, and biological domains.

My goal is to become someone who can connect ideas across disciplines and contribute wherever systems thinking is needed.
